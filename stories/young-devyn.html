In the Hood and on Disney

It all started when she was just 7 years old. At a school event to celebrate Caribbean heritage in New York, Devyn Tyson got up on a stage for the first time. Representing Trinidad and Tobago, where her family is from, she surprised even her mom, who was amazed by her confidence with the microphone.

“I had literally like an out-of-body experience,” recalled Natasha Mason-Tyson in an interview for Drill Down. “I was like: 'Whose kid is this?'”

That kid became Young Devyn, first a Soca singer as a child and now a rising star in New York's hip-hop scene. After transitioning from the traditional Trinidian genre to rap, her cultural background is still an important part of who she is on stage.

“Every time you hear me rap, I always say 'Brooklyn/Trinidad,'” said Devyn. “So I always just make sure I represent them because they represented me first.”

Drill rap came into Devyn's career by chance, when she decided to post a freestyle video on social media. The raw video eventually became “Straight to It”, her top-performing song with more than 650,000 views on YouTube. While drill is often associated with violence, Young Devyn tries a different approach when experimenting with the subgenre. For instance, she doesn't curse.

“If you listen to my song, you might be like: 'Damn, she just said something crazy!' but I didn't curse,” she said. “I'm trying to be in the hood and on Disney.”
